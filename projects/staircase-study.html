Stairway Evacuation Study

A research project studying how stairway congestion affects movement, flow rate, and evacuation performance in multi-story buildings.

Project Overview

This research project focused on understanding how people move through stairways during emergency evacuations. Stairways are one of the main evacuation routes in multi-story buildings, especially when elevators are not available or cannot be used.


The study looked at how congestion, flow rate, and occupant density can affect evacuation performance. The goal was to better understand when stairway movement begins to slow down and how that impacts overall evacuation efficiency.

Research Objective

The objective was to study stairway flow under different usage conditions and compare observed movement behavior with published evacuation research. This helped create a foundation for future simulation work involving multi-story buildings and larger occupant loads.

Methodology

The project included a pilot observation study of stair movement at Fresno State, literature benchmarking, and early simulation development. Real-world observations were used to compare low-flow and high-flow conditions and examine how travel time changed as stair usage increased.

Key Findings

The study showed that higher stairway flow can increase travel time and reduce movement efficiency. Performance does not drop all at once; instead, early signs of congestion can appear before the stairway reaches severe crowding.


The results supported the idea that stairway performance depends on demand, occupant movement speed, stair geometry, and interaction between individuals.
