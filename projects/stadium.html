Stadium Design & Scale Model

A conceptual stadium developed in CAD and built as a physical model to explore form, seating, circulation, and large-scale design.

The Project

This project began with the challenge of designing a stadium that felt visually complete while still being practical to build as a scale model. I wanted the design to communicate the size and character of a real venue without making the physical model unnecessarily complicated.

I developed the stadium digitally in SolidWorks and then used 3D printing, laser cutting, and hand assembly to turn the design into a finished model.

Designing the Stadium

Stadium design involves more than shaping an exterior shell. The seating, field, entrances, circulation paths, roofline, and surrounding structure all need to feel connected.

I focused on creating a clear overall form and arranging the seating so the model would still read as a functioning stadium. I also considered how people would enter, move through, and experience the space, even though the project remained conceptual.

Designing at Model Scale

Some details that work in a full-size design become too small or fragile in a physical model. I had to simplify certain features while keeping the overall stadium recognizable and visually balanced.

From CAD to Physical Model

Before fabrication, I divided the digital model into parts based on how each one could be produced. Components with more complex geometry were better suited for 3D printing, while flatter pieces could be prepared for laser cutting.

This required thinking ahead about scale, material thickness, part fit, and assembly order. A component could look correct in SolidWorks but still be difficult to print, cut, or connect to the rest of the model.

Fabrication and Assembly

After the parts were produced, I assembled the stadium by hand. This stage involved fitting the seating areas, structural pieces, exterior elements, and base together while checking alignment throughout the build.

Small differences between the digital model and the fabricated parts became more noticeable during assembly. Some pieces required adjustment so that the final model would sit correctly and maintain its intended shape.

Balancing Form and Structure

One of the main ideas I explored was how a stadium can feel open and visually light while still appearing supported. Large venues often use repeating structural elements to create rhythm, carry loads, and define the architectural character of the building.

Although this was not a full structural design, developing the model made me think about span, support locations, symmetry, and how structural systems influence the appearance of a large public space.

What I Learned

This project taught me that physical models reveal problems that are easy to overlook on a computer screen. Part thickness, connection points, tolerances, and assembly order all became important once fabrication began.

It also helped me understand how design decisions affect the overall experience of a space. The seating, circulation, structure, and exterior form could not be developed as separate pieces because each one influenced the others.

My favorite part of the project was seeing the stadium move from a digital concept into something physical. That process made the design feel more real and gave me a better understanding of how ideas change as they move from modeling into fabrication.
