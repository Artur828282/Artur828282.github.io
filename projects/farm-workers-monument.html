Farm Workers Monument

A commemorative structure designed and built to honor the work, sacrifice, and lasting contributions of farm workers.

The Idea

This project began with the idea of creating something that would honor farm workers in a physical and lasting way. I wanted the structure to feel more personal than a simple sign or plaque, so the design combined a symbolic centerpiece with a bench that people could actually use.

The goal was to create a place for reflection while also recognizing the labor and contributions of farm workers in the community.

Design Decisions

The central eagle shape became the main visual element of the monument. It was designed to be recognizable from a distance while remaining simple enough to fabricate from steel.

I also had to think about how the frame, decorative pieces, and seating would work together as one structure. The design needed to feel balanced, remain stable, and be practical to assemble with the tools and materials available.

More Than a Display Piece

Adding the bench changed the project from something people would only look at into a space they could sit beside, gather around, and spend time with.

Building the Monument

1

Plan the Structure

Develop the overall form, seating layout, steel frame, and decorative elements before fabrication began.

2

Cut the Steel Components

Use plasma cutting to create the eagle, maple leaves, and other decorative pieces from steel plate.

3

Assemble the Frame

Fit and weld the steel members together while checking alignment, spacing, and overall stability.

4

Build the Seating

Cut, finish, and install the wood components that form the bench and complete the gathering space.

5

Finish and Assemble

Bring the steel and wood elements together, review the final appearance, and complete the monument for display.

Working Through Fabrication

One of the main challenges was turning a symbolic idea into something that could actually be fabricated. Some details that looked good in the design had to be simplified so they could be cut cleanly, welded securely, and assembled without losing the original meaning.

The project also required switching between steelwork and woodworking. Each material behaved differently, so the design had to account for how the frame would be welded, how the bench would be supported, and how the finished pieces would fit together.

What I Learned

This project taught me that fabrication often improves a design by forcing you to make practical decisions. Dimensions, connections, material thickness, and assembly order all became more important once I started building the structure.

It was also one of the first projects that showed me how engineering and fabrication can carry cultural and community meaning. The finished result was not only something I built, but something meant to represent and honor other people.
