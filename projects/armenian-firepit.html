Armenian Firepit

A functional steel firepit inspired by Tsitsernakabert, created to connect remembrance, cultural identity, and hands-on fabrication.

The Idea

I wanted to create something that was useful as an everyday object but still carried personal and cultural meaning. The design was inspired by Tsitsernakabert, the Armenian Genocide Memorial in Yerevan.

Rather than copying the monument directly, I used its recognizable form as the starting point for a smaller steel structure that could function as a firepit while still representing remembrance and resilience.

Adapting the Memorial

The original memorial was designed at a monumental scale, so its geometry could not simply be reduced and used without changes. I had to decide which parts of the form were most important and how they could be adapted into a compact fabricated piece.

The sloped steel panels became the main feature of the design. Their arrangement creates the familiar inward-facing shape while also forming an enclosed space for the fire.

Keeping the Meaning Without Copying the Monument

The goal was not to make a miniature replica. I wanted the finished piece to feel inspired by Tsitsernakabert while still working as its own functional design.

From CAD to Fabrication

1

Study the Original Form

Review the geometry and symbolism of Tsitsernakabert and identify the elements that made the memorial recognizable.

2

Develop the CAD Design

Create the overall shape, panel dimensions, angles, and base layout before beginning fabrication.

3

Prepare the Steel Pieces

Cut the sheet-metal components with a shear and prepare the edges and surfaces for assembly.

4

Fit and TIG Weld the Structure

Position the panels, check their spacing and angles, and TIG weld the pieces together into the final form.

5

Review the Finished Piece

Check the overall alignment, stability, appearance, and function of the firepit after fabrication.

Working With Steel

The design depended on several similar panels meeting at consistent angles. Small differences in cutting or positioning could make the final shape appear uneven, so fit-up was an important part of the build.

TIG welding allowed me to create cleaner and more controlled joints, but it also required careful preparation and steady alignment. Because the panels were visible from every side, both the structure and appearance of the welds mattered.

Function and Symbolism

The fire gives the design an additional connection to remembrance. At night, the light appears between the steel panels and makes the form more visually similar to a memorial space.

At the same time, the piece remains functional. It can be used as a firepit rather than existing only as a display object, which was an important part of the original idea.

What I Learned

This project taught me how much care is needed when translating a symbolic idea into a fabricated object. The design still needed to be practical, stable, and possible to build with the available tools.

It also helped me become more comfortable moving between CAD, sheet-metal preparation, fit-up, and welding. Each stage affected the next, and small inaccuracies became much more visible once the panels were assembled.

What made the project especially important to me was that the final piece had a purpose beyond fabrication practice. It allowed me to connect technical work with my Armenian heritage in a way that felt personal and lasting.
